## Insight 1: The Channel Projection Is Lossy Compression, and the Loss Function Must Be Channel-Aware

**Category:** System Modeling

**One-liner:** Transforming a rich canonical product record into channel-specific representations (WhatsApp's 5,000-char limit, Instagram's 2,200-char caption, marketplace's rigid attribute schema) is fundamentally a lossy compression problem where what you discard must be optimized per channel—not a generic truncation.

**Why it matters:** The naive approach to multi-channel product syndication treats all channels as subsets of the canonical record: truncate the description to fit, resize the image, and push. But lossy compression that simply truncates from the end loses the SEO-critical keywords placed in the second paragraph (web descriptions front-load context, but buyers scroll), the unique selling proposition buried in a long description (WhatsApp shoppers need the hook in the first line since they see a preview), and the product specification details that marketplace search algorithms index for discoverability. The production channel projection engine must understand what each channel values: web descriptions prioritize keyword density and long-form SEO content; WhatsApp descriptions prioritize the first 100 characters (preview visible without "read more") and conversational tone; Instagram captions prioritize hashtags and visual language; marketplace listings prioritize structured attributes that drive search ranking. This means the "compression" isn't a simple truncation—it's a re-generation with channel-specific objectives. The system maintains channel-specific content generation prompts that produce optimized content for each channel from the same set of product attributes, rather than compressing a single master description. This re-generation approach costs 3× more in LLM inference than truncation but produces 40% higher click-through rates on WhatsApp and 25% better search ranking on marketplaces.

## Insight 2: Inventory Safety Buffers Are a Hidden Dynamic Programming Problem Across Channels

**Category:** Atomicity

**One-liner:** Allocating inventory safety buffers across N channels to minimize total overselling risk while maximizing total listing availability is a constrained optimization problem where the optimal buffer per channel depends on every other channel's buffer—not a static 10% per channel.

**Why it matters:** The naive approach is to apply a fixed safety buffer per channel: if you have 100 units, list 90 on each channel (10% buffer). But this means you've effectively listed 90 × N units across N channels when you only have 100 total, which is fine when N=2 but creates massive overselling risk when N=5. The alternative—dividing inventory across channels (20 units on each of 5 channels)—eliminates overselling but dramatically reduces listing availability and creates artificial stockouts on channels where the product is most popular. The production system models this as a dynamic allocation problem: buffer per channel is computed as a function of (1) the channel's historical sales velocity for this product (fast-selling channels get lower buffers because stock is more likely to actually sell), (2) the channel's sync latency (WhatsApp with 30-second sync gets a smaller buffer than a marketplace with 5-minute sync, because shorter sync latency means less time for concurrent overselling), (3) current total stock level (at 10 units total, buffers must be aggressive; at 1,000 units, buffers can be minimal), and (4) the cost of overselling vs. the cost of delisting (a fashion item that's oversold results in a refund and lost customer; a commodity item that's oversold can be cross-shipped). This allocation is recomputed every time inventory changes, creating a feedback loop where each sale triggers reallocation across all channels.

## Insight 3: The AI Theme Selection Decision Graph Creates an Implicit Contract with the Merchant That Is Harder to Maintain Than the Theme Itself

**Category:** Workflow

**One-liner:** When the AI selects a theme, color palette, and layout based on product image analysis, it creates hundreds of interdependent aesthetic decisions—and allowing the merchant to override one decision (e.g., changing the color) without cascading effects through the decision graph requires treating the AI's output as a decomposable dependency graph, not a monolithic design.

**Why it matters:** Traditional theme systems have no dependency problem: the merchant picks a theme, picks a color, picks a font—each choice is independent. When the AI selects the theme, it makes correlated decisions: the blue color palette was chosen because the product images have warm tones and blue provides complementary contrast; the serif font was chosen because the category is "traditional clothing" and serif fonts convey craft and heritage; the product grid layout with large images was chosen because the image quality is high (AI detected sharp, well-lit photos). If the merchant overrides the color from blue to yellow, the complementary contrast relationship is broken—the product images now clash with the background. If the system silently accepts the override, the storefront looks worse than a random template would. If the system re-derives the entire theme from the new color, the merchant loses all other customizations they liked. The production system maintains a decision dependency graph for each AI-generated theme: `{color_palette → [hero_background, button_color, text_contrast, footer_style], product_images → [layout_type, image_size, grid_columns], category → [font_family, tone_of_voice, homepage_sections]}`. When the merchant overrides a node, only its dependents are re-evaluated, and only if the override creates a constraint violation (contrast ratio < 4.5:1, image-background clash score > threshold). This selective re-evaluation preserves merchant intent while maintaining visual coherence—a capability that no traditional template system needs but that is essential for trust in AI-driven design.

## Insight 4: Dynamic Pricing Cold-Start for New Products Is a Multi-Armed Bandit Problem Disguised as a Regression Problem

**Category:** System Modeling

**One-liner:** Setting the initial price for a new product with zero sales history appears to be a regression problem (predict optimal price from product attributes), but in production it is a multi-armed bandit problem where each price point is an arm, the reward is margin × conversion rate, and the exploration budget is the merchant's tolerance for experimentation.

**Why it matters:** The regression approach—train a model on existing products' attributes and successful prices, predict the optimal price for a new product—fails because the relationship between product attributes and optimal price is non-stationary and merchant-specific. A handmade kurta from a premium brand commands 3× the price of an identical-looking kurta from an unknown merchant, but the product attributes (material, color, style) are identical. The brand premium is invisible to the attribute-based model. The bandit approach recognizes that the only way to discover the right price is to test prices and observe conversion rates. But unlike a traditional A/B test (which requires thousands of visitors for statistical significance), an SME store might get 20 visitors/day. The production system implements a contextual bandit with Thompson sampling: the prior distribution is set from category-level regression (the regression model is the prior, not the answer), and the posterior is updated with each visitor interaction (view, add-to-cart, purchase, bounce). With a tight prior from category data, the bandit converges to near-optimal pricing within 100-200 visitors instead of the 1,000+ needed for a flat A/B test. The exploration strategy is constrained by the merchant's configured "price flexibility" setting (conservative: ±5%, moderate: ±15%, aggressive: ±25%) to prevent the system from testing prices that would alarm the merchant or their customers.

## Insight 5: CDN Cache Invalidation for 3 Million Storefronts Requires Product-to-URL Dependency Tracking That Resembles a Build System's Dependency Graph

**Category:** Caching

**One-liner:** When a product is updated, the CDN must invalidate not just the product page but every page that references that product (homepage featured section, category page, search results, cross-sell widgets on other product pages)—and computing this invalidation set for 3 million storefronts without over-invalidation requires a dependency graph between products and URLs that mirrors a build system's dependency tracking.

**Why it matters:** The naive cache invalidation approach is to purge the entire storefront's CDN cache when any product changes. This is correct but wasteful: a store with 500 product pages, 50 category pages, and a homepage would require purging 551 URLs when a single product's price changes, even though only 3-5 pages actually display that product. At platform scale (500,000 product updates/day across 3 million stores), the naive approach generates 275 million URL purge requests per day—overwhelming any CDN provider's purge API. The production system maintains a product-to-URL dependency index: when a storefront page is generated, the renderer records which products appear on that page (`product_xyz → [/products/silk-kurta, /category/ethnic-wear, /]`). When a product is updated, only the URLs in its dependency set are invalidated. This reduces purge volume by 95%+ (from 551 URLs to ~5 URLs per product update). The dependency index is updated incrementally during page regeneration (when a category page is regenerated and now includes a different set of products, the index is updated). This mirrors how build systems track source-to-artifact dependencies: a change to `product.js` only triggers rebuild of pages that import it, not the entire site. The index itself is stored in a fast key-value store, keyed by product ID, with TTL-based expiry to prevent stale entries for deleted products.

## Insight 6: Payment Gateway Routing Is Not Just Cost Optimization—It Is a Real-Time Reliability Problem Where Cost and Reliability Signals Are Anti-Correlated

**Category:** Resilience

**One-liner:** The cheapest payment gateway for a given payment method is often the one with the worst real-time success rate (because it's cheapest due to less investment in infrastructure), creating a Pareto tension where the cost-optimal route and the reliability-optimal route diverge—and the routing algorithm must dynamically trade off cost savings against conversion loss.

**Why it matters:** Static gateway routing based on published fee schedules (Gateway A charges 0.3% for UPI vs. Gateway B's 0.5%) ignores the real-time success rate dimension. In production, the cheapest gateway experiences more frequent degradations: lower fees attract higher volume, which creates congestion; less revenue per transaction means less investment in redundancy. During peak hours (8-10 PM), the cheapest UPI gateway's success rate might drop from 97% to 88%, while the more expensive gateway maintains 96%. At an average order value of ₹800, a 9% success rate difference means ₹72 of lost GMV per 100 attempts—far exceeding the ₹1.60 fee difference (0.2% × ₹800). The production routing algorithm maintains a real-time scoring function that combines cost weight (30%) with trailing 5-minute success rate (40%) and latency (20%) and reliability track record (10%). During stable periods, the cheapest gateway wins. During degradation, traffic automatically shifts to the more expensive but more reliable gateway. The challenge is that the trailing success rate is a lagging indicator—by the time 5 minutes of data shows degradation, hundreds of payments have already failed. The system supplements trailing metrics with a "canary payment" probe: every 60 seconds, a synthetic ₹1 payment is attempted through each gateway (and immediately refunded). If the canary fails, the gateway is proactively downgraded before production payment failures accumulate.

## Insight 7: The Merchant's First 5 Minutes Determine 6-Month Retention, Making Store Creation a Latency Problem With Retention as the SLO, Not Response Time

**Category:** Cost Optimization

**One-liner:** The store creation latency SLO (< 3 minutes) exists not because merchants are impatient about waiting but because merchants who don't see a live, professional-looking store within the first session have an 80% churn rate within 7 days—making creation speed an indirect retention metric where every additional minute of latency has a quantifiable LTV cost.

**Why it matters:** The engineering team's instinct is to treat store creation latency as a UX performance metric: faster is better, but 3 minutes vs. 5 minutes doesn't matter much. Production cohort analysis tells a different story. Merchants who complete store creation in the first session (defined as a single uninterrupted usage period before the app is backgrounded) have a 6-month retention rate of 62%. Merchants who leave during store creation and return later have a 6-month retention rate of 14%. The critical insight is that the completion threshold is not a fixed time but a function of perceived progress: merchants tolerate 5 minutes if they see continuous progress (images being analyzed, descriptions appearing, theme being customized), but abandon after 90 seconds of apparent inactivity (spinner with no visible change). This transforms the latency problem from "make it faster" to "make it feel progressive." The production system implements streaming progress updates: the visual analyzer processes images one at a time and immediately shows the analysis result (detected category, extracted colors) in the UI. Description generation streams token by token to the preview. Theme customization applies incrementally (first color, then typography, then layout). Each of these visible steps takes 5-10 seconds, but the continuous visual progression maintains merchant engagement through a 3-minute process that would cause abandonment if shown as a single loading spinner. The engineering cost of streaming every pipeline stage to the UI is 30% higher than batch processing, but the 4× difference in retention makes it the most cost-effective engineering investment in the system.

## Insight 8: Multi-Channel Order Attribution Is a Causal Inference Problem, Not a Last-Click Tracking Problem

**Category:** Data Structures

**One-liner:** When a customer discovers a product on Instagram, researches it on the merchant's website, adds it to cart on WhatsApp, and completes purchase via a UPI payment link—attributing this order to a single channel (last-click: WhatsApp) misinforms the merchant's channel investment decisions, but proper multi-touch attribution for SMEs with 20 orders/day requires probabilistic models that work with tiny sample sizes.

**Why it matters:** Enterprise attribution tools (multi-touch attribution models) require thousands of conversion paths to train statistical models. An SME with 20 orders/day and 3 channels has roughly 3³ = 27 possible attribution paths, but only 20 data points per day—making per-merchant multi-touch attribution statistically meaningless. Last-click attribution (the default) systematically overvalues WhatsApp (where most Indian SME purchases finalize) and undervalues Instagram (where most discovery happens), leading merchants to stop investing in Instagram content—which then reduces overall sales because the discovery channel has dried up. The production system implements hierarchical Bayesian attribution: individual merchant data is supplemented with category-level priors from the platform's aggregate data (millions of orders across thousands of similar merchants). A fashion merchant with limited data inherits attribution patterns from the fashion category's aggregate model (which has strong priors about Instagram's discovery role), while their merchant-specific data adjusts the posterior distribution. This approach gives meaningful attribution insights even for merchants with 10 orders/day, while converging to merchant-specific patterns for larger sellers. The attribution model outputs channel contribution percentages (e.g., Instagram: 35% influence, Website: 25% influence, WhatsApp: 40% influence) rather than binary last-click assignment—presented to merchants as a simple "channel scorecard" in their daily WhatsApp business briefing.

## Insight 9: Incremental Static Regeneration at Platform Scale Creates a Thundering Herd on Origin When Product Updates Are Correlated

**Category:** Contention

**One-liner:** When the platform sends a promotional push notification to 10,000 merchants suggesting they update their festive pricing, and 3,000 merchants accept the suggestion within 10 minutes, each acceptance triggers page regeneration for 5-10 affected pages per store—creating 15,000-30,000 simultaneous regeneration requests that overwhelm the origin rendering infrastructure, even though each individual regeneration is lightweight.

**Why it matters:** Incremental static regeneration (ISR) works beautifully for uncorrelated updates: a merchant changes a price, 3-5 pages regenerate, the CDN is updated, done. The system is designed for the steady-state where product updates are uniformly distributed over time. But business events create correlated updates: festival season pricing changes, platform-wide promotions ("update your store for Diwali"), AI-driven bulk pricing recommendations accepted simultaneously, or a policy change requiring all stores to update their return policy page. When 3,000 stores each trigger 5 page regenerations within 10 minutes, the rendering service receives 15,000 requests in 10 minutes—250/min vs. the normal 50/min. The rendering service scales based on request rate, but the auto-scaler has a 3-5 minute reaction time, during which the queue grows and regeneration latency degrades from 5 seconds to 60+ seconds. During this lag, the CDN serves stale content, which is acceptable for price changes (brief stale price) but problematic for inventory changes (stale "in stock" for an out-of-stock product). The production system implements a regeneration priority queue with rate limiting: inventory-affecting changes get immediate high-priority regeneration (bypasses rate limit); price and content changes go through a rate-limited queue that smooths the burst over 30 minutes instead of 10. Additionally, the system detects correlated-update patterns proactively: when a bulk pricing recommendation is sent to 10,000 merchants, the rendering service pre-scales 3× in anticipation of the acceptance wave, rather than waiting for the auto-scaler to react.

## Insight 10: The Competitor Price Scraping Pipeline's Reliability Is Inversely Proportional to Its Value—The Products Worth Monitoring Are the Hardest to Scrape

**Category:** External Dependencies

**One-liner:** Competitor prices for commodity products (electronics, branded goods) are easily scrapable from structured marketplace listings, but these products have tight margins and little pricing flexibility—while the high-margin unbranded products (handmade crafts, local food) where pricing intelligence would be most valuable have no structured competitor listings to scrape.

**Why it matters:** The naive assumption behind the dynamic pricing engine is that competitor prices are available, structured, and refreshable on a 4-hour cycle. For branded products listed on major marketplaces, this is true: a Samsung Galaxy A15 has a scrapable price on every marketplace, updated in real-time. But branded products have manufacturer-suggested retail prices, razor-thin margins, and marketplace price-matching policies that make dynamic pricing nearly irrelevant—the price is essentially fixed by the brand. The products where dynamic pricing creates the most merchant value—unbranded fashion, artisanal goods, local food specialties—have no structured competitor listings. A handmade jute bag from a rural artisan has no "competitor price" to scrape; the closest comparable might be a different-looking bag from a different artisan on a different platform with a different quality level. The production system addresses this asymmetry by building category-level price distributions from platform-internal data (what are other merchants on this platform charging for similar products?) rather than relying solely on external scraping. For unbranded products, the "competitor" is not an external listing but a cluster of similar products in the platform's own catalog, identified by visual similarity (image embedding distance) and attribute overlap. This internal pricing intelligence is more reliable than scraping (100% availability, real-time updates) and more relevant than external prices (same merchant segment, same customer base). The external scraping pipeline becomes a supplementary signal for branded products only, reducing scraping infrastructure cost by 60% while improving pricing recommendation relevance for 75% of the product catalog.

## Insight 11: Multi-Tenant Database Shard Migration Is a Distributed Systems Problem Where the Migration Itself Can Cause the Outage It Is Trying to Prevent

**Category:** Contention

**One-liner:** When a viral merchant is automatically migrated from a shared shard to a dedicated shard, the dual-write migration process temporarily doubles the write load on the source shard—potentially degrading performance for co-located merchants during the exact period when the shard is already under stress.

**Why it matters:** The noisy-neighbor mitigation strategy for multi-tenant databases is conceptually simple: when a merchant exceeds traffic thresholds for 3 consecutive hours, migrate them to a dedicated shard. The migration uses a dual-write pattern: writes go to both the source shard and the new dedicated shard; once the new shard is caught up, reads switch over, then writes switch over, and the source shard's copy is cleaned up. The problem is timing. The migration is triggered because the source shard is under stress from the viral merchant's traffic. The dual-write phase doubles the write load from that merchant (each write goes to two shards). If the viral merchant is generating 500 writes/second (order processing, inventory updates, analytics events), the source shard now handles 500 additional writes/second during migration—exactly when it can least afford the extra load. The production system mitigates this with a two-phase approach: (1) immediate read-path relief—redirect the viral merchant's read traffic to a read replica that is promoted to serve only their reads, reducing read load on the source shard by 40-60% within minutes; (2) deferred write migration—the full dual-write migration is scheduled for the next off-peak window (2-4 AM), when the shard load has naturally decreased. If the shard is critically overloaded (90%+ capacity), the system instead activates read-only mode for non-critical operations (analytics, dashboard queries) for co-located merchants while the migration proceeds, accepting temporary degradation for some merchants to prevent cascading failure for all.

## Insight 12: The WhatsApp Business API's Catalog Update Rate Limit Creates an Invisible Priority Inversion Where Low-Value Updates Starve High-Value Ones

**Category:** Traffic Shaping

**One-liner:** WhatsApp Business API allows 80 catalog update requests per second across the platform's entire app, but does not differentiate between inventory updates (preventing overselling) and description updates (cosmetic improvement)—so during peak update periods, a queue of 10,000 description updates can delay 50 critical inventory updates by minutes, creating overselling risk on the highest-traffic channel.

**Why it matters:** Most channel APIs enforce a single rate limit across all update types. WhatsApp's 80 req/s limit applies equally to "update product inventory from 10 to 9" and "update product description from version A to version B." During steady-state, the 80 req/s limit is ample—the platform generates ~58 WhatsApp sync events per second on average. But during a content regeneration cycle (when the AI model is updated and all product descriptions are re-generated), the description update volume spikes to 200+ events/second, saturating the rate limit for 30+ minutes. During this window, inventory updates (which are time-critical for overselling prevention) queue behind thousands of description updates (which are time-insensitive). The naive rate limiter processes events in FIFO (First-In-First-Out, like a line at a store) order, meaning an inventory update submitted at minute 15 of the description wave doesn't execute until minute 25—a 10-minute delay on a metric with a 30-second SLO. The production system implements a priority-aware rate limiter with two queues: a high-priority queue for inventory and price events (guaranteed 60% of the rate limit budget = 48 req/s) and a low-priority queue for content and image events (remaining 40% = 32 req/s). When no high-priority events are pending, the low-priority queue can use the full 80 req/s. This priority split ensures inventory updates are never delayed by more than 1.25 seconds (1/48 × 60 = 1.25s per event) regardless of the content update backlog, while content updates still complete within a reasonable window.

## Insight 13: AI-Generated SEO Metadata Creates a Platform-Wide Duplicate Content Risk That Undermines Every Merchant's Search Ranking

**Category:** Search

**One-liner:** When an LLM generates product descriptions for 150 million products using the same model, prompt templates, and product attribute vocabulary, the resulting descriptions cluster into a small number of semantic patterns—and search engines penalize domains (or in this case, subdomains on a shared domain) with substantially similar content, degrading SEO for all merchants on the platform.

**Why it matters:** Search engines detect and penalize "thin content" and "duplicate content" across a site. When the platform hosts 3 million storefronts on subdomains of a shared domain (e.g., `merchant-name.platform.com`), search engines may treat these as parts of a single site. If the AI generates descriptions for 10,000 red cotton kurtas using the same model and prompt, the resulting descriptions will share 60-80% of their structure and vocabulary—enough for search engine deduplication to flag them as substantially similar. The first merchant indexed gets the canonical ranking; the remaining 9,999 are treated as near-duplicates and suppressed. This is catastrophic for a platform whose value proposition is "SEO-optimized storefronts." The production system combats this at three levels: (1) description diversification—the LLM prompt includes a random "writing style" parameter (narrative, technical, conversational, poetic, bullet-point, comparison) that varies across merchants, producing structurally distinct descriptions for identical products; (2) merchant-specific signals—the description incorporates the merchant's unique selling points (location, craftsmanship story, brand history) extracted during onboarding, making each description genuinely unique; (3) uniqueness scoring—every generated description is compared against a rolling similarity index of recent descriptions on the platform (locality-sensitive hashing for O(1) lookup), and descriptions with > 30% overlap with existing content are regenerated with a different style seed. This uniqueness enforcement costs 15% more in LLM inference (regeneration rate) but preserves the SEO value that justifies the platform's pricing tier.

## Insight 14: The Merchant's Product Image Quality Distribution Has a Fat Tail That Breaks the Visual Analyzer's Category Detection Accuracy

**Category:** Edge Computing

**One-liner:** The visual analyzer's product category detection accuracy is calibrated against professional product photography (clean background, good lighting, single product), but 40% of SME merchants upload images taken on smartphone cameras in cluttered environments—and the accuracy drop on low-quality images (from 92% to 64%) cascades into wrong theme selection, wrong SEO keywords, and wrong competitor set for pricing.

**Why it matters:** The AI store creation pipeline assumes the visual analyzer can accurately detect product category, color palette, and style from uploaded images. The model is trained on a dataset dominated by e-commerce product images (white background, centered product, good lighting) and achieves 92% top-1 category accuracy on this distribution. But SME merchants—especially first-time digital sellers—upload images taken on phone cameras in their shop or home: cluttered backgrounds, poor lighting, multiple products in one frame, watermarks from WhatsApp forwarding, and screenshots of competitor listings. On this "real-world SME" distribution, category detection accuracy drops to 64%, color extraction is corrupted by background colors, and style classification is meaningless. The cascade effect is severe: wrong category → wrong theme → wrong SEO keywords → wrong competitor set for pricing → pricing recommendations that are irrelevant. The production system implements a three-tier image quality classification: Tier 1 (professional quality, > 90% category confidence) → full AI pipeline; Tier 2 (acceptable quality, 70-90% confidence) → AI pipeline with explicit merchant confirmation of detected category before proceeding; Tier 3 (poor quality, < 70% confidence) → redirect merchant to the platform's built-in camera guide with tips for better product photos, offer to re-analyze after re-upload. Additionally, the background removal model runs before category detection for Tier 2 and 3 images, improving category accuracy on cluttered-background images from 64% to 81% at a cost of 2 additional seconds per image in the pipeline.

## Insight 15: The Merchant's Override Pattern Is the Highest-Signal Training Data for the Next Model Version, but Collecting It Requires Treating Every Override as a Structured Feedback Event

**Category:** Streaming

**One-liner:** When a merchant overrides an AI-generated title, changes a theme color, or rejects a pricing recommendation, the override encodes a preference signal that is more valuable for model improvement than any amount of labeled training data—but only if the system captures what was generated, what was changed, and the context of the change as a structured event rather than silently accepting the edit.

**Why it matters:** Traditional ML improvement relies on periodic model retraining with new labeled data. But in a zero-code storefront builder, the most valuable training signal is implicit in merchant behavior: the merchant who changes "Elegant Handcrafted Silk Kurta in Vermillion Red" to "Red Silk Kurta" is communicating that the AI over-elaborated; the merchant who changes the theme from "minimalist" to "vibrant" after the AI selected minimalist based on product images is communicating that the visual analyzer's style classification is wrong for their brand identity; the merchant who rejects a price recommendation of ₹899 and sets ₹1,299 is communicating that the competitor-based pricing undervalues their brand premium. Each of these overrides, captured as a structured feedback event `{original: X, override: Y, context: {product_category, merchant_tenure, store_traffic}}`, becomes a labeled training example for the next model version. The production system instruments every AI-generated field with change tracking: when a merchant edits any AI-produced content, the system records the original generation, the edit, the time-to-edit (immediate edits suggest the AI was clearly wrong; delayed edits suggest evolving preference), and whether the merchant has overridden similar content before (pattern detection). This override corpus grows at 50,000 events/day and is the primary input to the monthly model retraining pipeline. The key architectural insight is that the override tracking must be invisible to the merchant—any friction (like a "why did you change this?" prompt) would reduce override quality by biasing toward stated rather than revealed preferences.
